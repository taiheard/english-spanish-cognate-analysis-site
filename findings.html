Key findings

Insights that reshape language learning strategy

False friends, loanword flows, complexity metrics, and historical timing all play a deeper role than surface similarity suggests.

1. The big picture

Most similarities are safe—but the dangerous ones appear frequently enough to demand attention.

While most similar words are safe, 1 in 13 will trick you.
  • True cognates represent 86.8% of the dataset, providing a powerful foundation for accelerated vocabulary learning.
  • False friends account for 7.4% of word pairs yet show up often in daily language, creating outsized confusion risk.
  • Loanwords illustrate the cultural and technological forces directing vocabulary exchange between languages.

2. The false friends paradox

The most deceptive words are also the ones learners encounter first.

Abstract concepts drift apart; concrete terms stay aligned.

Frequency paradox

  • False friends recorded a mean frequency score of 2.95, significantly higher than true cognates (p < 0.000001, Cohen's d = -0.64).
  • High-frequency false friends such as actual/actual and embarazada/embarrassed demand explicit instructional warnings.

Domain risk analysis

  • Emotions/Psychology (13.33%) and Family/Kinship (20.00%) have the highest false friend rates.
  • Technology/Tools (3.69%) remains one of the safest domains thanks to modern standardization.

3. Loanwords: a two-way street

Cultural enrichment flows one way; technological necessity flows the other.

Direction matters. English loans = must-have modern vocabulary.

Spanish → English

  • Mean frequency score of 3.76 indicates lower everyday usage (specialty terms).
  • Culinary and cultural staples dominate: taco, fiesta, siesta.

English → Spanish

  • Mean frequency score of 2.84 reflects high-frequency, daily-use vocabulary.
  • Technology and digital life lead: email, software, smartphone.

4. Complexity & similarity

Surface similarity stops at the spelling—complexity lives elsewhere.

Spanish-English Similarity is a poor proxy for complexity—r = 0.008.
  • Levenshtein similarity shows almost no correlation with overall complexity (r = 0.008).
  • Word length and syllables strongly align with complexity (r ≥ 0.76).
  • Health and medicine cognates trend most complex; nature and everyday communication trend simpler.

5. Temporal patterns

False friends are relics of history—modern standardization curbs new divergence.

Most false friends are ancient—they've been tricking learners for centuries.
  • 55.3% of false friends emerged before 1400; 31.8% in the Renaissance; only 5.3% in modern times.
  • Transition periods (1400-1500, 1700-1800) show distinctive blends of semantic drift types.
  • Modern divergence often ties to domain-specific technical language rather than organic drift.

6. Business impact

Data-driven strategies for language learning applications and curriculum design.

Strategic targeting delivers maximum impact with minimal resources.
  • Prioritizing high-FFR domains (Family/Kinship, Emotions/Psychology) could significantly reduce learner confusion in critical vocabulary areas.
  • Targeting high-frequency false friends first has disproportionate impact on daily usage scenarios learners encounter most often.
  • Resource allocation efficiency: strategic focus on the highest-risk vocabulary addresses the majority of common errors.

Curriculum optimization

  • Early-stage instruction should flag high-frequency false friends (actual, embarazada, sensible) with explicit warnings.
  • Domain-based sequencing: introduce safe domains (Technology/Tools) before high-risk domains (Family, Emotions).
  • Leverage true cognates (86.8% of dataset) for rapid vocabulary expansion while maintaining false friend awareness.

Application design

  • Adaptive learning systems can prioritize false friend practice based on frequency data and domain risk.
  • Error prediction models benefit from domain-specific false friend rates and historical emergence patterns.
  • Personalized learning paths can sequence vocabulary by length and syllable complexity to match individual learner readiness.
